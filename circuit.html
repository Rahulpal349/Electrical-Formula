2. Definitions & Classifications

Network vs. Circuit

Hover to compare

Network: Any interconnection of electrical elements (may or may not be closed).

Circuit: A network containing at least one closed path for current to flow.

Active vs. Passive

⚡ vs ▱

Passive: Only consumes or stores energy (R, L, C).

4. Resistor Fundamental Formulas

Resistance from Material Properties

$$ R = \frac{\rho \ell}{A} $$
  • $\rho$ = Resistivity of material (Ω·m)
  • $\ell$ = Length of conductor (m)
  • $A$ = Cross-sectional area (m²)
Stretching & Compressing

When wire is STRETCHED to $n$ times its length:

$$ R' = n^2 R $$

When wire is COMPRESSED to $1/n$ of its length:

$$ R' = \frac{R}{n^2} $$

Original Resistance: R

Series & Parallel Combinations

Series Combination
$$ R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} R_i $$
Parallel Combination
$$ \frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n} $$

For two resistors:

$$ R_{eq} = \frac{R_1 \times R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \quad \text{(product over sum)} $$

For $n$ equal resistors:

$$ R_{eq} = \frac{R}{n} $$

6. Resistance vs Temperature

Resistance changes with temperature according to the Temperature Coefficient of Resistance ($\alpha$).

$$ R_t = R_0[1 + \alpha_0(T - T_0)] $$
  • $R_t$ = Resistance at temp T, $R_0$ = Rest. at ref. temp T₀
  • $\alpha$ = Temp. coefficient (per °C)
$$ \alpha_T = \frac{\alpha_0}{1 + \alpha_0 T} $$

Temp. coefficient at any other temperature.

Nature of Coefficients (PTC/NTC)
  • α > 0 (PTC): Metals. Resistance increases with T. (e.g., Cu, Al).
  • α < 0 (NTC): Semiconductors/Insulators. Resistance decreases with T. (e.g., Si, Ge).
  • α ≈ 0: Alloys. Near-zero change. (e.g., Manganin, Eureka).

7. Resistivity & Cable Insulation Resistance

Specific Resistance (Resistivity $\rho$)

Resistance of a unit length and unit cross-section of a material.

$$ R = \rho \frac{\ell}{A} $$
  • Depends on the nature of the material and temperature.
  • Does not depend on length or area.
  • Unit: $\Omega \cdot m$
Insulation Resistance of a Cable
$$ R_{ins} = \frac{\rho}{2\pi \ell} \ln\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right) $$

Notice: $R_{ins} \propto \frac{1}{\ell}$

c) Capacitors & Capacitance

Capacitor formulas, series-parallel combinations, delta-star transforms & transient response.

11. Capacitor Formulas

(a) Basic Parallel Plate Capacitance

$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A}{d} \quad \text{Farad} $$
  • $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}$ F/m
  • $\varepsilon_r$ = relative permittivity
  • $A$ = plate area (m²)
  • $d$ = distance between plates (m)
$$ C \propto A, \quad C \propto \frac{1}{d}, \quad C \propto \varepsilon_r $$
C = 100 pF

(b) Layered Dielectrics (Series)

Different dielectrics stacked horizontally between plates.

$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{\dfrac{t_1}{\varepsilon_{r1}} + \dfrac{t_2}{\varepsilon_{r2}} + \dfrac{t_3}{\varepsilon_{r3}}} $$

Acts as capacitors in SERIES:

$$ \frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} $$
(c) Vertical Division (Parallel)
$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A_1}{d} + \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A_2}{d} $$

$C = C_{air} + C_{dielectric}$

(d) Horizontal Division (Series)
$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d - t + t/\varepsilon_r} $$

$\frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{C_{air}} + \frac{1}{C_{dielectric}}$

(e) Cylindrical Capacitor

$$ C = \frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r \ell}{\ln(b/a)} $$
  • $a$ = inner radius
  • $b$ = outer radius
  • $\ell$ = length
  • $E_{max} = \frac{V}{a\ln(b/a)}$

(f) Multi-plate Variable

$$ C = \frac{(n-1)\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r A}{d} $$

Where $n$ = total number of plates

12. Series & Parallel Capacitor Combinations

Series Combination

$$ \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \dots + \frac{1}{C_n} $$

For two: $C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2}$

  • Charge (Q) is SAME on each capacitor
  • $V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3$
  • Voltage divider: $V_1 = V \frac{C_2}{C_1+C_2}$
Ceq = 3.33 µF

Parallel Combination

$$ C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \dots + C_n = \sum C_i $$

  • Voltage (V) is SAME on each capacitor
  • $Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3$
  • Charge divider: $Q_1 = Q_{total} \frac{C_1}{C_{eq}}$
Ceq = 30.00 µF

Note: Capacitor combinations are OPPOSITE to resistor rules!

$$ \text{Series Caps: } C_{eq} < C_{min} \quad | \quad \text{Parallel Caps: } C_{eq}> C_{max} $$

14. Capacitor Responses & Transients

Key Behaviors
  • Initial state ($t=0$): Uncharged capacitor acts as a SHORT CIRCUIT.
  • Steady state ($t=\infty$): Fully charged capacitor acts as an OPEN CIRCUIT (blocks DC).
  • Instantaneous Change: Voltage across capacitor cannot change instantaneously. $V_C(0^-) = V_C(0^+)$.
$$ i_c(t) = C \frac{dv_c(t)}{dt} $$
$$ v_c(t) = \frac{1}{C} \int i_c(t) dt $$
Energy Stored
$$ E = \frac{1}{2} C V^2 = \frac{1}{2} Q V = \frac{Q^2}{2C} $$

Charging & Discharging (RC Circuit)

Current $\tau$: 3.0 s
Charging: $V_C = V_S(1-e^{-t/\tau})$

15. Important Points: Capacitor Behaviour

(a) Voltage Opposition
Capacitor OPPOSES change of voltage
$$ V_C(0^+) = V_C(0^-) $$ (voltage cannot jump instantaneously)
(b) Energy Storage
Stores energy in ELECTRIC FIELD
$$ E = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}QV \text{ J} $$
(c) Charging
$I_C(t)$ must be POSITIVE
$$ I_C(t) = C\frac{dV_C}{dt} \Rightarrow \frac{dV_C}{dt} > 0 \Rightarrow I_C > 0 $$
(d) Discharging
$I_C(t)$ must be NEGATIVE
$$ I_C(t) = C\frac{dV_C}{dt} \Rightarrow \frac{dV_C}{dt} < 0 \Rightarrow I_C < 0 $$
(e) Fixed Polarity
DC voltages NEVER change polarity
While capacitor charges/discharges
(f) Live Energy
$E = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$
Assumes $C = 1\text{ F}$

16. Inductor Fundamentals

Faraday's Law for Inductor

$$ V_L = L\frac{di}{dt} \quad \text{and} \quad i(t) = \frac{1}{L}\int i\,dt + i(0^-) $$
Where $V_L$ = Voltage (V), $L$ = Inductance (Henry), $\frac{di}{dt}$ = Rate of current change (A/s)

(a) Coefficient of Self Inductance ($L$)

$$ L = \frac{N\phi}{i} = \frac{V_L}{di/dt} = \frac{N^2}{\mathcal{S}} = \frac{\mu_0\mu_r N^2 A}{\ell} \text{ Henry} $$
$N$ = Number of turns
$\phi$ = Magnetic flux (Wb)
$\mathcal{S}$ = Reluctance
$A$ = Core area (m²)
$\ell$ = Coil length (m)
$\mu_0 = 4\pi\times10^{-7}$ H/m
Induced $V_L$: 50 V

(b) Coefficient of Mutual Inductance ($M$)

$$ M = \frac{N_2\phi_1}{i_1} = \frac{V_{L2}}{di_1/dt} = \frac{\mu_0\mu_r N_1 N_2 A}{\ell} \text{ Henry} $$
$$ \text{Coupling Factor, } K = \frac{M}{\sqrt{L_1 L_2}} \quad (0 \leq K \leq 1) $$ $$ M_{max} = \sqrt{L_1 L_2} \quad \text{(when } K=1 \text{)} $$
Mutual Inductance $M = K\sqrt{L_1L_2}$

(b) Current Opposition
OPPOSES sudden change of current
$$ I_L(0^+) = I_L(0^-) $$ (current cannot jump instantaneously)

Inductor is an ENERGY STORAGE element

23. RLC Complete Response Summary

Source-Free Natural Response — All Three Cases
■ Overdamped ($\zeta > 1$) ■ Critically Damped ($\zeta = 1$) ■ Underdamped ($\zeta < 1$)
Series RLC (Source-Free)
$$ \alpha = \frac{R}{2L}, \quad \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} $$
Overdamped ($\alpha > \omega_0$, $C > \frac{4L}{R^2}$):
$i(t) = A_1 e^{s_1 t} + A_2 e^{s_2 t}$
Critical ($\alpha = \omega_0$, $C = \frac{4L}{R^2}$):
$i(t) = (A_1 + A_2 t)e^{-\alpha t}$

$i(t) = e^{-\alpha t}(A_1\cos\omega_d t + A_2\sin\omega_d t)$

$v(t) = A_1 e^{s_1 t} + A_2 e^{s_2 t}$
Critical ($\alpha = \omega_0$, $L = 4R^2C$):
$v(t) = (A_1 + A_2 t)e^{-\alpha t}$

29. Voltage Source Connections

Series Connection

Adding (aiding — same polarity):

$$ V_1 \text{ series } V_2 \rightarrow V_{eq} = V_1 + V_2 $$

Subtracting (opposing — opposite polarity):

$$ V_1 \text{ series } (-V_2) \rightarrow V_{eq} = V_1 - V_2, \quad V_1 > V_2 $$

Series with Resistance:

$$ I = \frac{V_{eq}}{R} = \frac{V_1 + V_2}{R} $$
$V_{eq} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} V_k$ (Algebraic sum)
Parallel Connection
In parallel, VOLTAGE SOURCE is dominant

Two equal sources: $V_1 \parallel V_2 = V$

Parallel with resistance: $V \parallel R \rightarrow V_{eq} = V$

Unequal parallel: Violates KVL! (Not allowed)

Short circuit: $V \parallel 0 \approx 0 \text{ V}$ (Source shorted)

Electrical Measurements

Measurement methods, instrument classification, static characteristics, error analysis & statistical treatment.

a) Measurement Fundamentals

Methods, instrument types, and performance characteristics

1. Methods of Measurement

Fundamental

1. Direct Method: Unknown quantity is directly compared against a standard.

$$ \text{Reading} = \text{Instrument Scale Value} $$

Inaccurate — direct comparison may have errors. E.g.: Ruler, Ammeter reading.

2. Indirect Method: Unknown quantity computed from measured related quantities.

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} \quad \text{(measure V and I separately)} $$

Accurate, more sensitive, preferred method. E.g.: Power P = VI.

$$ \text{Direct} \rightarrow \text{Inaccurate, Simple} \quad;\quad \text{Indirect} \rightarrow \text{Accurate, Sensitive} $$

2. Classification of Instruments

3 Classes

(i) Absolute vs Secondary:

Absolute — value from instrument constants, no calibration. E.g.: Tangent galvanometer.
Secondary — must be calibrated. E.g.: Ammeter, Voltmeter.

(ii) Deflection vs Null:

Deflection — pointer shows magnitude; fast but less accurate. E.g.: PMMC, Moving iron.
Null — balance detected at zero; slow but high accuracy. E.g.: Potentiometer, Bridges.

(iii) Recording vs Integrating:

Recording — logs variation over time. E.g.: CRO, ECG.
Integrating — accumulates total. E.g.: Energy meter (kWh). $W = \int_0^T P\,dt$

8 Parameters

1. Accuracy:

$$ \% \text{Acc} = \left(1 - \frac{|A_m - A_t|}{A_t}\right) \times 100 $$

Closeness of measurements to each other (reproducibility).

3. Sensitivity:

$$ S = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta Q} = \frac{1}{I_{FSD}} \;\Omega/\text{V} $$

4. Repeatability / Reproducibility:

Short-term vs long-term consistency of readings.

5. Dead Time & Dead Zone:

$$ t_{dead} = \text{delay before response} $$

$$ \Delta x_{dead} = \text{input range with no output} $$

6. Linearity:

$$ \% e_{lin} = \frac{|y_{act} - y_{ideal}|}{y_{FSD}} \times 100 $$

7. Resolution:

4. Errors in Measurement

3 Types

Static Error: $$ \delta A = A_m - A_t $$

Relative Error: $$ \varepsilon_r = \frac{\delta A}{A_t} = \frac{A_m - A_t}{A_t} $$

Percentage Error: $$ \% \varepsilon = \frac{A_m - A_t}{A_t} \times 100 $$

Percentage Accuracy: $$ \% \text{Acc} = (1 - |\varepsilon_r|) \times 100 $$

Gross: Human mistakes (parallax, wrong reading).
Systematic: Instrument, environmental, observational errors.
Random: Statistical fluctuations — follow Gaussian distribution.

5. Limiting Error & Static Correction

Correction

Limiting Error:

$$ \delta A_{limit} = \pm \frac{\text{Class}\%}{100} \times \text{FSD} $$

Relative Limiting Error at reading:

$$ \varepsilon_r = \frac{\delta A_{limit}}{A_m} $$

Static Correction:

$$ \delta C = A_t - A_m = -\delta A $$

$$ A_t = A_m + \delta C \quad \text{(corrected value)} $$

Non-Linearity Error:

$$ \% e_{NL} = \frac{|O_{actual} - O_{ideal}|_{max}}{\text{FSD}} \times 100 $$

6. Combination of Quantities with Limiting Errors

Propagation

Sum / Difference:

$$ X = X_1 \pm X_2 $$

$$ \delta X = |\delta X_1| + |\delta X_2| $$

Absolute errors add directly.

Product / Quotient:

$$ x = \frac{x_1 \cdot x_2}{x_3} $$

$$ \frac{\delta x}{x} = \frac{\delta x_1}{x_1} + \frac{\delta x_2}{x_2} + \frac{\delta x_3}{x_3} $$

Relative errors add.

Composite (Power):

$$ x = x_1^a \cdot x_2^b \cdot x_3^c $$

$$ \frac{\delta x}{x} = a\frac{\delta x_1}{x_1} + b\frac{\delta x_2}{x_2} + c\frac{\delta x_3}{x_3} $$

Example: $ P = V^2/R \Rightarrow \frac{\delta P}{P} = 2\frac{\delta V}{V} + \frac{\delta R}{R} $

c) Statistical Analysis & Instrument Types

Mean, deviation, confidence intervals, and analog instrument classification

7. Arithmetic Mean & Deviation Analysis

Statistics

Arithmetic Mean: $$ \bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i $$

Deviation: $$ d_i = X_i - \bar{X} \quad;\quad \sum d_i = 0 $$

Standard Deviation: $$ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum d_i^2}{n-1}} \;\;(n < 20) \quad;\quad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum d_i^2}{n}} \;\;(n \geq 20) $$

Variance: $ V = \sigma^2 $   Probable Error: $ r = 0.6745\,\sigma $

Std Error of Mean: $$ \sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $$

$\pm\sigma \to 68.27\%$   $\pm 2\sigma \to 95.45\%$   $\pm 3\sigma \to 99.73\%$

8. Error Types & Analog Instruments

Classification

Gross Errors: Human mistakes — cannot be treated mathematically.

Systematic Errors: Instrument (friction, zero error), Environmental (temp, humidity), Observational (parallax).

$$ \delta R = R_0 \alpha \Delta T \quad \text{(resistance drift with temperature)} $$

Random Errors: Follow Gaussian distribution. Error reduces as $ \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} $
