0.2 Resistor Fundamental Formulas

Resistance from Material Properties

$$ R = \frac{\rho \ell}{A} $$
  • $\rho$ = Resistivity of material (Ω·m)
  • $\ell$ = Length of conductor (m)
  • $A$ = Cross-sectional area (m²)
Stretching & Compressing

When wire is STRETCHED to $n$ times its length:

$$ R' = n^2 R $$

When wire is COMPRESSED to $1/n$ of its length:

$$ R' = \frac{R}{n^2} $$

Original Resistance: R

Series & Parallel Combinations

Series Combination
$$ R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} R_i $$
Parallel Combination
$$ \frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n} $$

For two resistors:

$$ R_{eq} = \frac{R_1 \times R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \quad \text{(product over sum)} $$

For $n$ equal resistors:

$$ R_{eq} = \frac{R}{n} $$

0.4 Resistance vs Temperature

Resistance changes with temperature according to the Temperature Coefficient of Resistance ($\alpha$).

$$ R_t = R_0[1 + \alpha_0(T - T_0)] $$
  • $R_t$ = Resistance at temp T, $R_0$ = Rest. at ref. temp T₀
  • $\alpha$ = Temp. coefficient (per °C)
$$ \alpha_T = \frac{\alpha_0}{1 + \alpha_0 T} $$

Temp. coefficient at any other temperature.

Nature of Coefficients (PTC/NTC)
  • α > 0 (PTC): Metals. Resistance increases with T. (e.g., Cu, Al).
  • α < 0 (NTC): Semiconductors/Insulators. Resistance decreases with T. (e.g., Si, Ge).
  • α ≈ 0: Alloys. Near-zero change. (e.g., Manganin, Eureka).

0.5 Resistivity & Cable Insulation Resistance

Specific Resistance (Resistivity $\rho$)

Resistance of a unit length and unit cross-section of a material.

$$ R = \rho \frac{\ell}{A} $$
  • Depends on the nature of the material and temperature.
  • Does not depend on length or area.
  • Unit: $\Omega \cdot m$
Insulation Resistance of a Cable
$$ R_{ins} = \frac{\rho}{2\pi \ell} \ln\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right) $$

Notice: $R_{ins} \propto \frac{1}{\ell}$

0.9 Capacitor Formulas

(a) Basic Parallel Plate Capacitance

$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A}{d} \quad \text{Farad} $$
  • $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}$ F/m
  • $\varepsilon_r$ = relative permittivity
  • $A$ = plate area (m²)
  • $d$ = distance between plates (m)
$$ C \propto A, \quad C \propto \frac{1}{d}, \quad C \propto \varepsilon_r $$
C = 100 pF

(b) Layered Dielectrics (Series)

Different dielectrics stacked horizontally between plates.

$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{\dfrac{t_1}{\varepsilon_{r1}} + \dfrac{t_2}{\varepsilon_{r2}} + \dfrac{t_3}{\varepsilon_{r3}}} $$

Acts as capacitors in SERIES:

$$ \frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} $$
(c) Vertical Division (Parallel)
$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A_1}{d} + \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A_2}{d} $$

$C = C_{air} + C_{dielectric}$

(d) Horizontal Division (Series)
$$ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d - t + t/\varepsilon_r} $$

$\frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{C_{air}} + \frac{1}{C_{dielectric}}$

(e) Cylindrical Capacitor

$$ C = \frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r \ell}{\ln(b/a)} $$
  • $a$ = inner radius
  • $b$ = outer radius
  • $\ell$ = length
  • $E_{max} = \frac{V}{a\ln(b/a)}$

(f) Multi-plate Variable

$$ C = \frac{(n-1)\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r A}{d} $$

Where $n$ = total number of plates

0.10 Series & Parallel Capacitor Combinations

Series Combination

$$ \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \dots + \frac{1}{C_n} $$

For two: $C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2}$

  • Charge (Q) is SAME on each capacitor
  • $V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3$
  • Voltage divider: $V_1 = V \frac{C_2}{C_1+C_2}$
Ceq = 3.33 µF

Parallel Combination

$$ C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \dots + C_n = \sum C_i $$

  • Voltage (V) is SAME on each capacitor
  • $Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3$
  • Charge divider: $Q_1 = Q_{total} \frac{C_1}{C_{eq}}$
Ceq = 30.00 µF

Note: Capacitor combinations are OPPOSITE to resistor rules!

$$ \text{Series Caps: } C_{eq} < C_{min} \quad | \quad \text{Parallel Caps: } C_{eq}> C_{max} $$

3. Three-Phase Systems

Star connection features:

  • Has a Neutral point (N) allowing grounding.
  • Provides two voltage levels (Phase and Line).
  • Phase vs Line relations:
$$ V_L = \sqrt{3} V_{ph} $$
$$ I_L = I_{ph} $$

Delta connection features:

  • Used for long-distance transmission, no Neutral.
  • Higher starting torque for motors.
  • Phase vs Line relations:
$$ V_L = V_{ph} $$
$$ I_L = \sqrt{3} I_{ph} $$

Universal Three-Phase Power (Balanced):

$$ P = \sqrt{3} V_L I_L \cos\phi = 3 V_{ph} I_{ph} \cos\phi $$

Network Theorems

Powerful analytical tools for simplifying complex linear circuits.

1. Thevenin's Theorem

Any linear bidirectional circuit with energy sources and resistors can be replaced by a single Equivalent Voltage Source ($V_{th}$) in series with an Equivalent Resistance ($R_{th}$).

Step 1: The Original Circuit

Identify the linear network and the load resistor ($R_L$) where we want to find the current or voltage.

Step 2: Remove the Load

Temporarily remove the load resistor $R_L$ from the circuit, leaving an open circuit across terminals A and B.

Step 3: Find Open Circuit Voltage ($V_{th}$)

Calculate the open-circuit voltage across the terminals A and B. This voltage is $V_{th}$.

Step 4: Find Equivalent Resistance ($R_{th}$)

Turn off all independent sources (short voltage sources, open current sources) and calculate the resistance looking into terminals A and B.

Step 5: Thevenin Equivalent Circuit

Reconnect $R_L$ to the simple series equivalent circuit. Now you can easily calculate load current using $I_L = V_{th} / (R_{th} + R_L)$.

⚙️ Try It: Calculate Thevenin Equivalent

2. Norton's Theorem

The dual of Thevenin's. Simplifies a circuit to a single Equivalent Current Source ($I_N$) in parallel with an Equivalent Resistance ($R_N$).

$$ I_N = I_{sc} = \frac{V_{th}}{R_{th}} \quad \text{and} \quad R_N = R_{th} $$

⚙️ Try It: Thevenin $\leftrightarrow$ Norton Transform

3. Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

Maximum power is delivered from a linear network to a load when the load resistance equals the Thevenin equivalent resistance of the network.

$$ R_L = R_{th} \text{ (DC)} $$
$$ Z_L = Z_{th}^* \text{ (AC)} $$
$$ P_{max} = \frac{V_{th}^2}{4R_{th}} $$

Power curve peaking exactly across the $R_L = R_{th}$ point.

4. Superposition Theorem

In a linear network with multiple sources, the total response is the algebraic sum of the individual responses due to each source acting independently.

$$ V_{Total} = V_{S1} + V_{S2} + \dots + V_{Sn} $$
  • Voltage Sources killed by replacing with a Short Circuit (0V)
  • Current Sources killed by replacing with an Open Circuit (0A)

Reciprocity Theorem

In a linear, bilateral network containing a single source, the ratio of excitation (Source) to response (V or I) remains constant when their positions are interchanged.

$$ \frac{V_j}{I_k} = \frac{V_k}{I_j} $$

Tellegen's Theorem

For any lumped network following KVL and KCL, the sum of power delivered is exactly equal to the sum of power absorbed across all branches.

$$ \sum_{k=1}^{n} V_k I_k = 0 $$

(Conservation of Energy in Circuits)

Substitution Theorem

Any branch with known voltage V and current I can be replaced by an ideal voltage source V, or ideal current source I, without affecting voltages and currents in the rest of the network.

$$ Z = V/I \rightarrow \text{Source } V \text{ or } I $$

Compensation Theorem

Analyzes the effect of changing impedance by $\Delta Z$. The change in currents can be modeled by placing a compensating voltage source $V_c = -I \Delta Z$ in series with the modified branch.

$$ \Delta I = -\Delta Z \times \frac{I}{Z_{th} + \Delta Z} $$

4. Transient & Frequency Response

Analyze circuit behavior over time and frequency domains.

SUB-SECTION: Transient Analysis

FIRST ORDER — RC CIRCUIT

$$ v(t) = V_f + (V_i - V_f)e^{-t/\tau} $$
$$ \tau = RC \text{ (time constant)} $$

At $t = \tau$: reaches 63.2% of final value

FIRST ORDER — RL CIRCUIT

$$ i(t) = I_f + (I_i - I_f)e^{-t/\tau} $$
$$ \tau = L/R $$

SECOND ORDER — RLC CIRCUIT

$$ \frac{d^2v}{dt^2} + \frac{R}{L}\frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{v}{LC} = \frac{V_s}{LC} $$

Characteristic equation:
$s^2 + 2\alpha s + \omega_0^2 = 0$

$\alpha = \frac{R}{2L}$ (damping coeff)

$\omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$ (natural freq)

Cases:

  • Overdamped: $\alpha > \omega_0 \quad (\alpha^2 > \omega_0^2)$
  • Critically Damped: $\alpha = \omega_0$
  • Underdamped: $\alpha < \omega_0$
       $\rightarrow \omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \alpha^2}$ (damped freq)

SUB-SECTION: Frequency Response

SERIES RESONANCE

$$ \omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC} \quad\quad f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} $$

$$ Z_{min} = R \text{ (at resonance)} $$

$$ Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0 CR} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} $$
$$ BW = \omega_0 / Q = R/L $$

PARALLEL RESONANCE

$$ \omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC} \quad\quad Z_{max} = R \text{ (at res)} $$
$$ Q = \frac{R}{\omega_0 L} = \omega_0 CR = R\sqrt{\frac{C}{L}} $$
$$ BW = \omega_0 / Q = 1/RC $$

BANDWIDTH & Q FACTOR

$$ BW = f_2 - f_1 \text{ (half-power frequencies)} $$
$$ Q = f_0 / BW $$

At $f_1, f_2$: Power = $P_{max}/2 \quad (-3dB)$

SUB-SECTION: Laplace Transform

$$ \mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = F(s) = \int_0^\infty f(t)e^{-st}dt $$

5. Advanced Topics

Two-Port Networks, Magnetically Coupled Circuits, and Op-Amps.

5.1 Two-Port Networks

Z-Parameters (Open-Circuit)

Expresses port voltages in terms of port currents.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ V_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} Z_{11} & Z_{12} \\ Z_{21} & Z_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I_1 \\ I_2 \end{bmatrix} $$
  • $Z_{11} = \frac{V_1}{I_1} \Big|_{I_2=0}$ (Input Impedance)

Y-Parameters (Short-Circuit)

Expresses port currents in terms of port voltages. Inverse of Z matrix.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} I_1 \\ I_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} Y_{11} & Y_{12} \\ Y_{21} & Y_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ V_2 \end{bmatrix} $$
  • $Y_{11} = \frac{I_1}{V_1} \Big|_{V_2=0}$ (Input Admittance)

Transmission (ABCD) Parameters

Relates input variables ($V_1, I_1$) to output variables ($V_2, -I_2$). Excellent for cascaded networks.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ I_1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} V_2 \\ -I_2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Hybrid (h) Parameters

Used extensively in amplifier and transistor modeling.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ I_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} h_{11} & h_{12} \\ h_{21} & h_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I_1 \\ V_2 \end{bmatrix} $$

5.2 Operational Amplifiers & Mutual Inductance

Ideal Op-Amp Characteristics

  • Infinite Input Impedance: $I_+ = I_- = 0$
  • Zero Output Impedance: $R_{out} = 0$
  • Infinite Open-Loop Gain: $A \to \infty$
  • Virtual Short: $V_+ = V_-$ (with negative feedback)

Inverting Gain: $ -R_f/R_{in} $

Non-Inverting Gain: $ 1 + R_f/R_{in} $

Magnetically Coupled Circuits

When magnetic flux from one coil links with another coil, a mutually induced voltage is produced.

$$ V_1 = L_1 \frac{di_1}{dt} \pm M \frac{di_2}{dt} $$
$$ M = k\sqrt{L_1 L_2} $$

($k$ = Coefficient of Coupling, $0 \le k \le 1$)
