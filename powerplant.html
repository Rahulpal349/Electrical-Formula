📊 Power Plant Performance Parameters

1. Connected Load

Sum of ratings of all equipment connected to supply

2. Maximum Demand

Greatest demand of load on power station over a given period

3. Demand Factor

$$ \boxed{\text{DF} = \frac{\text{Max demand}}{\text{Connected load}}} \quad (< 1) $$

4. Average Load

$$ \text{Avg load} = \frac{\text{Total kWh}}{\text{Hours}} $$

5. Load Factor

$$ \boxed{\text{LF} = \frac{\text{Average load}}{\text{Maximum demand}}} \quad (< 1) $$

High LF → Better utilization

6. Diversity Factor

$$ \boxed{\text{DivF} = \frac{\sum \text{Individual max demands}}{\text{Simultaneous max demand}}} \quad (\geq 1) $$

7. Plant Capacity Factor

$$ \boxed{\text{PCF} = \frac{\text{Actual kWh}}{P_{rated} \times 8760\,h}} $$

8. Plant Use Factor

$$ \boxed{\text{PUF} = \frac{\text{kWh generated}}{P_{cap} \times \text{hrs used}}} $$

9. Reserve Capacity

$$ \text{Reserve} = \text{Plant cap} - \text{Max demand} $$

10. Units per Annum

$$ \text{Units} = \text{Avg load (kW)} \times 8760\,h $$

11. Cost of Generation

$$ \text{Cost/kWh} = \frac{\text{Total annual cost (₹)}}{\text{Total units (kWh)}} $$

Tariff Types:

1. Simple Tariff
Fixed rate per kWh
2. Flat Demand
Based on max demand only
3. Two-Part
$\text{Cost} = a\cdot MD + b\cdot kWh$
4. Three-Part
$\text{Cost} = a\cdot MD + b\cdot kWh + c$

💧 Hydro Power Plant: Fundamentals
"India's first hydro project: Darjeeling, 1897"
Water stored at high elevation flows through penstock → drives turbine + alternator → electrical energy.

Energy Conversion Chain:

$$ \text{Potential Energy }(mgh) \;\rightarrow\; \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\;\text{(Kinetic)} \;\rightarrow\; \text{Electrical Energy} $$
Water stored at height → Flows through penstock → Drives turbine + alternator

Power in Hydroelectric Plants:

$$ \boxed{P = 9.81\times10^{-3}\times W\,Q\,H\,\eta \;\;\text{kW}} $$
$$ \boxed{P = \frac{0.736}{75}\,W\,Q\,H\,\eta \;\;\text{kW}} $$
$$ \boxed{P = \frac{WQH\eta}{75} \;\;\text{HP}} $$

Where:

• $W$ = Specific weight of water = $9.81\times10^3\,\text{N/m}^3 \approx 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$
• $Q$ = Rate of flow (m³/s)    • $H$ = Head / height of fall (m)    • $\eta$ = Overall efficiency

Unit Conversions:

$$ 1\,\text{HP} = 0.746\,\text{kW} = 550\,\text{ft} \cdot \text{lb/s} $$

Factors Before Constructing:

  • 1.Capital cost of plant
  • 2.Capital cost of erecting & maintaining transmission line
  • 3.Cost of energy generation should be minimum

🌊 Classification by Water Flow Regulation

Three Types Based on Pondage/Regulation:

1. Run-off River (No Pondage)

  • ▸ Low capacity & seasonal
  • ▸ Water taken directly from river
  • ▸ No storage possible
  • ▸ Used only when water available
  • ▸ Mainly for irrigation

2. Run-off River (With Pondage)

  • ▸ Pondage = storage at plant
  • ▸ Copes with hourly–weekly fluctuations
  • ▸ Base load OR peak load
  • ▸ Depends on stream flow

3. Reservoir Plants

  • ▸ Water stored in big reservoir
  • ▸ Full control of water flow
  • ▸ Base or peak load as needed
  • ▸ Maximum flexibility

Classification by Water Head:

LOW Head ($H < 30\,\text{m}$)

Created near dam — no surge tank
Turbines: Francis, Propeller, Kaplan

MEDIUM Head ($30 < H < 300\,\text{m}$)

Forebay → penstock → turbine
Forebay acts as reservoir + surge tank
Turbines: Francis, Propeller, Kaplan

HIGH Head ($H > 300\,\text{m}$)

Steep valley dammed for storage
Surge tank before valve house
Reduces water hammer severity
Turbine: Pelton Wheel

Water Hammer Pressure:

$$ \boxed{\Delta P = \rho \cdot a \cdot \Delta v} $$

$a$ = wave velocity $= \sqrt{K/\rho}$, $K$ = bulk modulus of water

Surge tank reduces $\Delta P$ by absorbing pressure waves

⚡ Base Load vs Peak Load Plants
🔵 Base Load Plants
▪Take load on base portion of load curve
▪Usually large capacity
▪Work on nearly constant load
▪Operate at HIGH load factor
▪Unit cost of energy: LOW
▪Ex: Coal, Nuclear, Run-of-river
$$ LF_{base} = \frac{P_{avg}}{P_{max}} > 0.8 $$
🔴 Peak Load Plants
▪Supply peak — top of load curve
▪Smaller capacity, quick start-up
▪Run-off with pondage = peak load
▪Reservoir plants = peak load possible
▪Unit cost of energy: HIGH
▪Ex: Gas turbines, Pumped storage, Hydro
$$ LF_{peak} = \frac{P_{avg}}{P_{max}} < 0.3 $$

🌀 Water Turbine Classification

Classification by Type of Flow:

1. Axial flow — parallel to rotation axis
2. Inward radial flow — radially inward
3. Tangential/peripheral — tangent to runner
4. Mixed flow — radial inlet, axial outlet

Major Turbine Types:

⚙️ Pelton Wheel (Impulse)

Tangential/peripheral flow  |  High head (>300 m)
Water jets hit cup-shaped buckets. All pressure drop in nozzle (not runner).
$$ u_{opt} = \frac{v_{jet}}{2} \qquad \eta_{hyd} = \frac{2u(v_j-u)(1+\cos\beta)}{v_j^2} $$

🌀 Francis Turbine (Reaction)

Mixed flow (inward radial + axial)  |  Medium head (30-300 m)
Water enters radially, exits axially. Pressure drops across runner.

🔄 Kaplan Turbine (Reaction)

Axial flow (propeller type)  |  Low head (<30 m)
Adjustable runner blades. Best for large discharge, low head. High efficiency over wide range.

Specific Speed:

$$ \boxed{N_s = \frac{N\sqrt{P}}{H^{5/4}}} $$

$N_s$ = specific speed, $N$ = rpm, $P$ = power (HP), $H$ = net head (m)

Pelton: Ns = 10-50 Francis: Ns = 50-300 Kaplan: Ns = 300-900

Unit Quantities (Turbine Similarity):

Unit Speed

$$ N_u = \frac{N}{\sqrt{H}} $$

Unit Discharge

$$ Q_u = \frac{Q}{D^2\sqrt{H}} $$

Unit Power

$$ P_u = \frac{P}{D^2 H^{3/2}} $$

Impulse: All pressure converted to KE in nozzle → jet drives wheel. Runner at atmospheric pressure.
Reaction: Water pressure + velocity both act on runner. Pressure drops across runner.

Reaction Turbine:

$$ \text{Water pressure + velocity} \rightarrow \text{Combined action on runner} $$ $$ \text{Pressure drops across runner blades} $$

Module 2 — Thermal (Steam) Power Plant
🔥 Module 2 · Energy Flow & System Overview
A Thermal Power Plant converts chemical energy in fossil fuels (coal, gas, oil) to electrical energy through a heat→mechanical→electrical chain. The Rankine Cycle is the theoretical basis for steam plant operation.
Energy Conversion Chain
$$\text{Fuel (Chemical)} \xrightarrow{\text{Boiler}} \text{Steam (Thermal)} \xrightarrow{\text{Turbine}} \text{Shaft (Mech)} \xrightarrow{\text{Generator}} \text{Electricity}$$
Rankine Cycle Key Processes
$$1 \to 2:\;\text{Isentropic expansion in turbine}\quad W_T = h_1 - h_2$$
$$2 \to 3:\;\text{Constant-pressure heat rejection}\quad Q_{out} = h_2 - h_3$$
$$3 \to 4:\;\text{Isentropic compression in pump}\quad W_P = h_4 - h_3$$
$$4 \to 1:\;\text{Constant-pressure heat addition}\quad Q_{in} = h_1 - h_4$$
Cycle Efficiency
$$\eta_{Rankine} = \frac{W_T - W_P}{Q_{in}} = \frac{(h_1 - h_2) - (h_4 - h_3)}{h_1 - h_4}$$
$$\text{Heat Rate} = \frac{3600}{\eta_{overall}} \;\text{kJ/kWh}$$

Diesel electric stations serve as stand-by plants, peak load plants, mobile units, and emergency supply for cinemas, hospitals, etc. They offer higher thermal efficiency than steam plants but are limited to small power requirements due to high fuel cost.

$$\text{6. AC or DC Generators}$$

🌀 Module 4 · Gas Turbine Power Plant
In gas turbine plants, combustion gases expand directly in the prime mover — no steam generation needed. Based on the Brayton cycle, they offer fast startup, compact design, but high back-work ratio (40–80% of turbine output drives compressor).
Gas Turbine Operation
$$\text{Working gas expands} \rightarrow \text{Heat energy} \rightarrow \text{Kinetic energy} \rightarrow \text{Shaft work (reaction)}$$
$$\boxed{\eta_{GT,simple} = 32\text{–}38\%}$$
Brayton Cycle (Ideal Gas Turbine)
$$\text{1-2: Isentropic compression} \quad \text{2-3: Const. pressure heat addition}$$
$$\text{3-4: Isentropic expansion} \quad \text{4-1: Const. pressure heat rejection}$$
$$\boxed{\eta_{Brayton} = 1 - \frac{1}{r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}}}$$
$$r_p = \frac{P_2}{P_1}\;\text{(pressure ratio, typical 10–30)} \quad \gamma = 1.4\;\text{(air)}$$
Work & Back Work Ratio
$$W_T = \dot{m}c_p(T_3 - T_4) \quad W_C = \dot{m}c_p(T_2 - T_1) \quad W_{net} = W_T - W_C$$
$$\text{Back work ratio} = \frac{W_C}{W_T} \approx 40\text{–}80\% \quad \text{(much higher than steam turbine!)}$$
Fuels & Site Selection
$$CV_{natural\,gas} \approx 55{,}000\;\text{kJ/m}^3 \quad \text{Natural gas (methane) — most used}$$
$$\text{Site: Near load center} \cdot \text{Gas pipeline access} \cdot \text{Land availability} \cdot \text{Transport}$$
Merits / Demerits
$$\text{✓ Simple design} \cdot \text{High reliability} \cdot \text{Compact} \cdot \text{Low initial cost} \cdot \text{No standby losses}$$
$$\text{✗ Low net output} \cdot \text{Low efficiency} \cdot \text{Noisy operation}$$

A solar (photovoltaic) cell converts light energy directly into electricity via the photovoltaic effect. Photons absorbed by a semiconductor create electron-hole pairs, separated by the p-n junction to produce current. Cells connect in series for voltage, parallel for current.

$$\boxed{E_{solar} = 1.6 \times 10^{18}\;\text{kWh/year (Earth receives)}}$$
$$G \approx 1000\;\text{W/m}^2\;\text{(peak, clear sky)} \quad G_{avg} \approx 200\text{–}250\;\text{W/m}^2\;\text{(India avg)}$$

$$\boxed{P_{cell} = V_{OC} \times I_{SC} \times FF}$$
$$V_{OC} \approx 0.5\text{–}0.7\;\text{V} \quad I_{SC}\;\text{(A)} \quad FF \approx 0.7\text{–}0.85\;\text{(fill factor)}$$

$$\eta_{cell} = \frac{P_{max}}{G \times A_{cell}} \quad \eta_{Si} \approx 15\text{–}22\% \quad \eta_{multi\text{-}junction} \approx 40\%+$$

$$\text{1. Light absorption} \rightarrow \text{2. e-h pair generation} \rightarrow \text{3. Charge separation}$$
$$\rightarrow \text{4. External circuit extraction} \rightarrow \text{5. Battery storage} \rightarrow \text{6. Load (utility)}$$

📈 Module 4.2 · Solar Cell I-V & P-V Characteristics
The solar cell I-V curve defines output — from short circuit current (I_sc) at V=0 to open circuit voltage (V_oc) at I=0. The Maximum Power Point (MPP) is where P = V×I is maximized. Fill Factor measures how close to ideal the cell operates. Shadow effects dominate in series configuration.
I-V Characteristic
$$\boxed{I = I_L - I_0\left[\exp\!\left(\frac{qV}{nkT}\right) - 1\right]}$$
$$\text{At } V=0:\; I_{sc} = I_L \quad \text{At } I=0:\; V_{oc} = \frac{nkT}{q}\ln\!\left(\frac{I_L}{I_0}+1\right)$$
$$V_{oc} \approx 0.5\text{–}0.7\;\text{V (per Si cell)} \quad I_{sc} \propto G \quad V_{oc} \propto \ln(G)$$
P-V & Maximum Power Point
$$P = V \times I = V\left[I_L - I_0\!\left(e^{qV/nkT}-1\right)\right]$$
$$\frac{dP}{dV} = 0 \Rightarrow \text{Maximum Power Point (MPP)} \quad \boxed{P_{mp} = I_{mp} \times V_{mp}}$$

🏭 Module 4.2 · Solar Cell Power Plant
Solar power plants convert sunlight to electricity at scale. Three system types exist: ON Grid (exports excess to grid, no battery), OFF Grid (fully independent with battery), and Hybrid (battery + grid — best reliability). Panel sizing depends on daily load, peak sun hours, and system efficiency.
Solar Energy Scale
$$\text{Sun radiates: } 3.5 \times 10^{26}\;\text{kW} \quad \text{Earth: } 2 \times 10^{17}\;\text{kW}$$
$$\boxed{P_{harness} \approx 2 \times 10^{13}\;\text{kW}} \quad \text{≡ burning 17 million tonnes of coal}$$
System Types
$$\text{i. ON Grid:} \quad \text{Solar} \rightarrow \text{Inverter} \rightarrow \text{Transformer} \rightarrow \text{Meter} \rightarrow \text{Grid}$$
$$P_{export} = P_{generated} - P_{consumed} \quad \text{(net metering, no battery)}$$
$$\text{ii. OFF Grid:} \quad \text{Solar} \rightarrow \text{Charge controller} \rightarrow \text{Battery} \rightarrow \text{Inverter} \rightarrow \text{Load}$$
$$\text{iii. Hybrid:} \quad \text{ON grid + OFF grid} \quad \text{Priority: Solar} \rightarrow \text{Battery} \rightarrow \text{Grid}$$
System Sizing
$$\boxed{P_{panel} \geq \frac{E_{daily}}{H_{peak} \times \eta_{system}}} \quad H_{peak} \approx 4\text{–}6\;\text{hrs/day} \quad \eta_{sys} \approx 0.75$$
$$\boxed{C_{bat} = \frac{E_{daily} \times \text{Autonomy days}}{DOD \times \eta_{bat} \times V_{bat}}} \quad DOD = 0.5\text{–}0.8$$
Components

🌞 Types of Solar Collectors

Non-Tracking (T < 100°C): Flat Plate, Evacuated Tube, CPC — stationary collectors.
Tracking (T > 100°C): 1-axis (Fresnel, Parabolic Trough, Cylindrical) and 2-axis (Solar Tower, Dish Stirling, Circular Fresnel).
CR = Aaperture/Areceiver. Higher CR → higher temperature → higher Carnot efficiency.
$$Q_u = \dot{m}c_p(T_{out}-T_{in}) = F_R[G\alpha - U_L(T_{in}-T_a)]\cdot A_c$$
$$\boxed{\eta_c = \frac{Q_u}{G\cdot A_c} = F_R\left[\alpha - \frac{U_L(T_{in}-T_a)}{G}\right]}$$
$$CR = \frac{A_{aperture}}{A_{receiver}} \qquad T_{max} = T_a + \frac{G\cdot CR\cdot\alpha}{U_L}$$

🏭 Solar Thermal Power Plant

🪞 Heliostats & Solar Tower

Heliostats: Flat or slightly curved mirrors with 2-axis tracking (azimuth + elevation) that reflect sunlight onto a central tower receiver.
Aheliostat ≈ 20–150 m². Large plants use hundreds to thousands of heliostats.
Tower receiver: 200–300m height, T ≈ 500–1000°C. CR ≈ 300–1500.
1 kW peak power requires 8 m² area (at G = 1000 W/m², η = 12.5%).
$$P_{tower} = G_{DNI}\times A_{total}\times\eta_{optical} \qquad \eta_{optical} \approx 50\text{-}70\%$$
$$\eta_{tower,Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_{cold}}{T_{hot}} = 1 - \frac{300}{800} = 0.625$$
$$CR_{tower} = \frac{\text{Total heliostat area}}{\text{Receiver area}} \approx 300\text{-}1500$$
$$\boxed{1\,\text{kW peak} = 8\,\text{m}^2 \text{ (PV at } \eta=12.5\%\text{)}}$$
Drawbacks of Solar Energy:
$$G_{avg} \approx 0.1\text{-}0.3\,\text{kW/m}^2 \text{ (24hr avg)} \quad\text{| Available only sunrise to sunset}$$
$$G_{cloudy} \approx 0.1\text{-}0.3 \quad vs \quad G_{clear} \approx 0.8\text{-}1.0\,\text{kW/m}^2$$
