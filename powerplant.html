2.4 Component Formulas

Boiler Efficiency

$$ \eta_{boiler} = \frac{m_s (h_s - h_{fw})}{m_f \times C_v} \times 100\% $$
$m_s$: steam gen, $h$: enthalpy, $m_f$: fuel, $C_v$: cal. value

Factor of Evaporation

$$ F_e = \frac{h_s - h_{fw}}{2257} $$

Economizer Temp Efficiency

$$ \eta_{eco} = \frac{T_{fw2} - T_{fw1}}{T_{g1} - T_{fw1}} \times 100\% $$

Steam Turbine Power

$$ P_{turbine} = m_s (h_1 - h_2) \text{ kW} $$

2.7 Efficiencies & Cooling Tower

Plant Performance

$$ \eta_{thermal} = \frac{P_{output} \times 3600}{m_f \times C_v} \times 100\% $$
$$ HR = \frac{3600}{\eta_{thermal}} \text{ kJ/kWh} $$
$$ SR = \frac{3600}{h_1 - h_2} \text{ kg/kWh} $$

3.2 Nuclear Fission & Energy

Mass-Energy Equivalence

$$ E = mc^2 $$

Chain Reaction & Four Factor

$$ k = \frac{\text{Neutrons in gen. } n+1}{\text{Neutrons in gen. } n} $$

MODULE 4 — Hydro Power Stations

4.2 Hydro Power Plant Layout & Performance

Power Equation

$$ P_{output} = \eta_{overall} \times 9.81 Q H \text{ (kW)} $$
Q = flow (m³/s), H = net head (m)

Specific Speed ($N_s$)

$$ N_s = \frac{N\sqrt{P}}{H^{5/4}} $$
$N_s < 60$: Pelton (Impulse)
$60 < N_s < 400$: Francis (Reaction)
$300 < N_s < 900$: Kaplan (Axial)

Pumped Storage

$$ \eta_{ps} = \eta_{pump} \times \eta_{turbine} $$
$$ E_{stored} = \rho g V_{water} H $$

MODULE 5 — Diesel Electric Power Stations

5.1 4-Stroke Engine Principles

1. INTAKE

Engine Power Forms

$$ \text{I.P.} = \frac{P_m L A N k}{60} $$
I.P = Indicated Power, $P_m$ = mean eff. pressure, $N$ = RPM ($N/2$ for 4-stroke), $k$ = cylinders
$$ \text{B.P.} = \frac{2 \pi N T}{60} $$
B.P = Brake Power (Actual output at shaft), $T$ = torque
$$ \text{F.P.} = \text{I.P.} - \text{B.P.} $$
F.P = Friction Power

5.3 Plant Layout & Auxiliaries

Efficiencies & Fuel

$$ \eta_{mech} = \frac{\text{B.P.}}{\text{I.P.}} \times 100\% $$
$$ \eta_{thermal} = \frac{\text{B.P.} \times 3600}{m_f \times C_v} $$

Specific Fuel Consumption (SFC)

$$ \text{SFC} = \frac{\text{Fuel consumed (kg/hr)}}{\text{Brake Power (kW)}} $$
Lower SFC implies better efficiency.

MODULE 6 — Gas Turbine Power Plants

6.1 Brayton Cycle & Efficiency

Thermal Efficiency

$$ \eta_{Brayton} = 1 - \frac{1}{(r_p)^{\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma}}} $$
$r_p = P_2/P_1$ = Pressure ratio, $\gamma = C_p/C_v$

Optimum Pressure Ratio (Max Work)

$$ r_{p,opt} = \left(\frac{T_3}{T_1}\right)^{\frac{\gamma}{2(\gamma-1)}} $$

6.2 Gas Turbine Layout (Open Cycle)

Work Ratio

$$ W_R = \frac{\text{Net Work}}{\text{Turbine Work}} = \frac{W_T - W_C}{W_T} $$
Unlike steam turbines, $W_C$ (compressor work) takes up ~50-60% of $W_T$, resulting in a low work ratio.

MODULE 7 — Non-Conventional Energy

7.1 Solar Power (PV & Thermal)

PV Cell Performance

$$ FF = \frac{V_m I_m}{V_{oc} I_{sc}} $$
FF = Fill Factor, $V_m, I_m$ = max power point, $V_{oc}$ = open circuit voltage, $I_{sc}$ = short circuit current
$$ \eta_{PV} = \frac{V_m I_m}{P_{in} A} \times 100\% = \frac{V_{oc} I_{sc} FF}{I_{rr} A} \times 100\% $$
$I_{rr}$ = Solar Irradiance ($W/m^2$)

Interactive I-V Curve

7.3 Wind Power

Wind Power Equation

$$ P = \frac{1}{2} \rho A v^3 C_p $$
$\rho$ = air density (~$1.225 \text{ kg/m}^3$), $A$ = swept area ($\pi r^2$), $v$ = wind speed, $C_p$ = coeff. of performance.

Betz Limit

$$ C_{p,max} = \frac{16}{27} \approx 0.593 \text{ (59.3\%)} $$
Max theoretical efficiency of a wind turbine.

7.7 Tidal & Biomass

Tidal Energy (Potential)

$$ E = \frac{1}{2} \rho A g R^2 $$
$R$ = Tidal range (head), $A$ = basin area, $\rho$ = seawater density (~$1025 \text{ kg/m}^3$). Works on two-way generation.

Biomass & Biogas

Biomass energy is extracted through direct combustion, gasification, or anaerobic digestion.
$$ \text{Biogas } \approx 55\text{-}70\% \text{ Methane (CH}_4\text{)} $$
$$ \text{Cal. Value } \approx 20 \text{-} 25 \text{ MJ/m}^3 $$

MODULE 8 — Economics of Power Generation

8.1 Load Curves & System Factors

Essential Factors

$$ \text{Load Factor } (LF) = \frac{\text{Average Load}}{\text{Max Demand}} = \frac{\text{Units Generated in } T}{\text{Max Demand} \times T} $$
Usually < 1. Higher is better for economics.
$$ \text{Capacity Factor } (CF) = \frac{\text{Average Load}}{\text{Plant Capacity}} = \text{LF} \times \frac{\text{Max Demand}}{\text{Plant Capacity}} $$
Indicator of reserve capacity unused.
$$ \text{Diversity Factor} = \frac{\sum \text{Individual Max Demands}}{\text{Simultaneous Max Demand of system}} $$
Always > 1. Helps lower overall capital cost.
$$ \text{Plant Use Factor} = \frac{\text{Actual Energy Generated}}{\text{Plant Capacity} \times \text{Hours in Use}} $$

8.2 Tariffs & Power Factor Penalty

Two-Part Tariff (Hopkinson Demand Rate)

$$ C = a \times (\text{kW}) + b \times (\text{kWh}) $$
$a$ = Charge per kW of Max Demand (Fixed cost recovery)
$b$ = Charge per kWh of energy consumed (Running cost recovery)

Power Factor Tariff

Low power factor requires larger kVA rating for the same kW, increasing capital cost of alternator/transformers and increasing $I^2R$ copper losses.
$$ \text{kVA tariff: } C = a \times \text{kVA}_{max} + b \times \text{kWh} $$
$$ \text{Since } \text{kVA} = \frac{\text{kW}}{\cos\phi} $$
A direct penalty is applied if PF drops below a threshold (e.g., 0.85).

8.5 Depreciation Calculation

Straight Line Method

$$ d = \frac{P - S}{n} $$
$P$ = Initial cost, $S$ = Salvage value, $n$ = useful life in years.
Equal amount written off every year.

Sinking Fund Method

$$ q = \frac{(P - S) \cdot r}{(1+r)^n - 1} $$
$q$ = Annual deposit into a fund earning interest rate $r$.

MODULE 9 — Interconnected Power Systems

9.1 Base vs Peak Load Plants

Base Load Plants Peak Load Plants
● Nuclear Power ● Gas Turbines
● Large Coal/Thermal ● Pumped Storage Hydro
● Run-of-river Hydro ● Diesel Sets
Operate at high LF. Slow to start but cheap to run. Operate at low LF. Quick to start, expensive to run.

Advantages of Interconnection

  • Exchange of peak loads (flattening the curve).
  • Use of older, less efficient plants primarily for peaks.
  • Increased diversity factor reduces required combined capacity.
  • Improved system reliability and stability.
  • Economic operation (merit-order dispatch).
